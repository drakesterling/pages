Thus far (photo sequence 1-231) is the extension of classical descriptive morphology to cover the Peculiars. The descriptors mostly classify the effects of distortions to classic mainline features, such as distortions to the spiral arms. The morphology extends the Hubble sequence.

Now begins a new section on interactions. From this point forward, a schema is sought to classify interactive phenomena and features, rather than a schema for describing individual galaxies (even when involved in an interaction).

Arp continues to use some old terminology for simplicity. For instance, filamentation is still called by the same name, but now it applies to an interactive pair.

As throughout this atlas, conservative ambiguity is maintained, as seen in the very first grouping, "Appearance of fission." This is not to suggest evolutionary sequence. Ironically, Arp's later research will furnish data supportive of fission. It is true that several of these photos suggest fission, for a couple of reasons which we will be pointing out as we go. There is no need to "make a case" for such an evolutionary model. Such evidence is provided in the second part of this book, presenting discoveries published subsequent to the atlas.

The classification of Double Galaxies is somewhat arbitrary, with several features being distributed outside of the named category. Thus, "Connected arms" is a major feature, but not limited to the category by that name. Here are the categories:

[xii] 1. LSB (6) 001-006

The first Arp galaxy is misplaced. It is a subtle armature displacement.

The next four Arp galaxies are simply Irregulars.

The sixth - the "Bearpaw" - is simply Amorphous.

One of the major classification vectors is found in those two words, Irregular and Amorphous. The first is a term for the Magellanic Clouds, whose irregularity is essentially sparseness - lacking clear spiral or bar shape, no nucleus, rather like a globular cluster gone to seed, really. Small, non-assertive. In a word, irregular. De Vaucouleurs adopted it and adapted it to his schema - in my opinion, a good move, but rather restricted. Amorphous was a term adopted later - another good term, this one a bit more generic, although somebody did try to squeeze an A-class into the Hubble sequence.

These are terms for true late-stage galaxies; instead of "early" and "late" I suggest diffusion.

Diffusion is a huge term. It's like colorization: suddenly galaxies have style, gradient. Galaxies grow old and disintegrate. We have seen, it would seem, many examples, nearly offering a complete morphology. Galaxies begin, grow, and attain various "perfect" forms; then they fade, disintegrate, dissolve, and disperse. We will see lots of examples of how the latter happens in the Arp collection. There are kinds of disintegration. They are most fascinating.

Interaction yields distortions, including disintegration; often combined in these photos. However, disintegration occurs independently anyway.

Dissolution and amorphousness must be distinguished at the outset. This class of galaxies is terminal, not mainline.

There is a whole classification system for dissolving galaxies. This class must be distinguished from mainline galaxies. Dissolution is not the same as diffusion. Dissolution is the vanishing of features, the death of a galaxy. Diffusion is a feature of interaction.
